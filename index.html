Understanding Breast Cancer

Breast cancer occurs when cells in the breast grow uncontrollably. It is the most common cancer among women worldwide, but with early detection, the survival rate is remarkably high.

Prevention Tips

While not all breast cancers can be prevented, these steps can significantly reduce your risk.

Regular Screening

Get annual mammograms starting at age 40 or earlier if you have risk factors. Early detection is the best protection.

Healthy Diet

Eat plenty of fruits, vegetables, and whole grains. A Mediterranean diet has been shown to lower breast cancer risk.

Regular Exercise

Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate activity per week. Exercise helps regulate hormones and boost immunity.

Avoid Smoking

Smoking is linked to a higher risk of breast cancer, especially in premenopausal women. Quit today for a healthier tomorrow.

Limit Alcohol

Even small amounts of alcohol can increase risk. Limit intake to no more than one drink per day for better health.

Maintain Healthy Weight

Being overweight or obese increases breast cancer risk, especially after menopause. Stay active and eat well.

Self-Examination Guide

Perform a breast self-exam once a month. Follow these simple steps to check for any changes.

Look at your breasts with arms at your sides, then raised above your head. Check for any changes in shape, size, skin texture, or nipple position. Look for dimpling, puckering, or redness.
Gently squeeze each nipple between your thumb and index finger. Check for any discharge — clear, milky, or bloody. Report any unusual discharge to your doctor.
Using the pads of your three middle fingers, press firmly in small circular motions. Cover the entire breast from collarbone to bra line, and from armpit to cleavage. Use light, medium, and firm pressure.
Place a pillow under your right shoulder. Use your left hand to examine your right breast in the same circular pattern. Repeat on the other side. Lying down spreads tissue evenly, making it easier to detect lumps.
Don't forget your armpits! Breast tissue extends into this area. Feel for any lumps or thickening. If you find anything unusual, contact your healthcare provider right away.

Risk Factors

Understanding your risk factors helps you make informed decisions about screening and prevention.

BRCA1 & BRCA2 Mutations

Inherited mutations in BRCA1/BRCA2 genes significantly increase lifetime breast cancer risk to 45–72%.

Family History

Having a first-degree relative (mother, sister, daughter) with breast cancer nearly doubles your risk.

Personal History

A previous diagnosis of breast cancer increases the risk of developing a new cancer in the other breast.

Alcohol Consumption

Women who drink 2–3 alcoholic beverages daily have a 20% higher risk compared to non-drinkers.

Physical Inactivity

A sedentary lifestyle contributes to weight gain and hormonal imbalances that increase cancer risk.

Poor Diet & Obesity

Excess body fat, especially after menopause, increases estrogen levels and breast cancer risk.

Early Menstruation

Starting periods before age 12 increases exposure to estrogen and progesterone over a lifetime.

Late Menopause

Going through menopause after age 55 means longer exposure to reproductive hormones.

Hormone Therapy

Long-term use of combined hormone replacement therapy (HRT) increases breast cancer risk.
